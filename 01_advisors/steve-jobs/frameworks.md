# Steve Jobs — Decision Frameworks

## 1. Simplify

**Principle:** Remove everything that is not essential. The question is always "what can we kill?"

**Source:** When Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, the company had 350 products across dozens of confusing categories. He drew a 2x2 matrix on a whiteboard — consumer and pro on one axis, desktop and portable on the other — and killed everything that did not fit into one of those four quadrants. Revenue rose. Margins expanded. The company survived.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. If you could only ship one feature, which one would it be? Ship that.
2. What would happen if you removed this entirely? If the answer is "nothing much," remove it.
3. Can a new customer understand what this does in ten seconds? If not, it is too complicated.
4. Are you adding this because it makes the product better, or because a competitor has it?
5. What is the cost of this complexity — in engineering time, support burden, and user confusion?

**How to Apply:** List every feature, product, or initiative. Force-rank them. Cut the bottom 70%. Do not negotiate. Do not keep things "just in case." The things you kill make the things you keep stronger. Revisit this exercise quarterly.

## 2. User Experience Audit

**Principle:** Would the customer love this? Not tolerate it. Not find it adequate. Love it. Start from the experience you want and work backward to the technology.

**Source:** The original iPhone began not with a technology assessment but with a vision: "What if you could just touch the screen and interact directly with your content?" The entire hardware stack was invented to serve that experience. Apple Stores began with the experience Jobs wanted — "a place where you walk in and feel something" — and the architecture followed.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. Describe the ideal customer experience in one sentence. Does your current design deliver that?
2. Walk through the first 60 seconds a new user encounters. Where is the friction? Where is the delight?
3. What would make someone show this to a friend? If there is no answer, the product is not good enough.
4. Are you starting from what is technically feasible, or from what the experience should feel like?
5. Close your eyes and use the product. What feels wrong?

**How to Apply:** Physically walk through the entire user journey, from first awareness through daily use. Document every moment of friction, confusion, or ugliness. Then redesign from the experience backward. Do not accept "the technology doesn't support that" as a final answer — that is the starting point for engineering, not the end.

## 3. Category Creation

**Principle:** Do not compete in existing categories. Create new ones. When you compete, you are playing someone else's game by someone else's rules. When you create a category, you define the rules.

**Source:** The iPod was not positioned as an MP3 player. It was "1,000 songs in your pocket." The iPhone was not positioned as a smartphone. It was "an iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator" — three products in one that created something none of them were. The iPad was not positioned as a tablet computer. It was a new category of device that sat between the phone and the laptop.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. What existing category would people put this in? That is the category you need to transcend.
2. Can you describe what this does without referencing any existing product? If not, you are not thinking big enough.
3. What is the behavior this enables that was previously impossible? Lead with that.
4. If you win this existing market, does that even matter? Or is the real opportunity the adjacent market that does not exist yet?

**How to Apply:** Stop benchmarking competitors. Stop building feature comparison matrices. Instead, ask: "What is the job the customer is trying to do, and what would a perfect solution look like if no product existed yet?" Design that. Name the category yourself. Define the criteria for success. Make competitors respond to your frame, not the other way around.

## 4. Full Stack Control

**Principle:** Own the entire experience from hardware to software to services. When you control the whole stack, you can make decisions no one else can — and deliver experiences no one else can deliver.

**Source:** Apple's vertical integration versus Microsoft's horizontal licensing model. Microsoft had 95% market share but could not control the user experience because it depended on Dell, HP, and others for hardware. Apple had 5% market share but owned the entire experience — and eventually the 5% was worth more than the 95%. The iPhone could deliver a seamless experience because Apple controlled the chip, the screen, the OS, the app store, and the services.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. Where in your stack do you depend on a third party? What does that dependency cost you in experience quality?
2. If you controlled one more layer, which layer would have the greatest impact on the customer experience?
3. Where do your competitors lose quality because they do not own the full stack?
4. What would vertical integration cost you, and is the experience improvement worth that cost?
5. Are you building a product or a platform? If a platform, where is the boundary between what you own and what partners own?

**How to Apply:** Map your entire value chain from raw inputs to customer experience. Identify the layers where quality degrades because a third party controls them. Evaluate whether bringing those layers in-house would meaningfully improve the product. The goal is not to own everything for the sake of it — the goal is to own everything that touches the customer experience.

## 5. Taste Test

**Principle:** Is this remarkable? Would you be proud to show this to someone you respect? Great companies ship products that make people feel something. If it does not evoke an emotional reaction, it is not done.

**Source:** Jobs personally approved every major Apple advertisement, every product design, every packaging decision. He held up prototypes and asked a simple question: "Is this the best we can do?" If the answer was anything other than an unhesitating yes, the team went back to work. "1,000 songs in your pocket" was not a feature description — it was a feeling. The click wheel was not a navigation mechanism — it was a delight.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. Would you use this yourself? Not "would you buy this" — would you use it every day and love it?
2. Pick up the product and hold it. Does it feel like something you are proud of?
3. Show it to the smartest person you know. Do their eyes light up?
4. In one sentence, what is the emotional reaction this product creates?
5. If you saw a competitor ship this exact thing, would you be worried or would you shrug?

**How to Apply:** Treat every decision as a design decision. The font on the receipt matters. The weight of the box matters. The sound the device makes when it turns on matters. Develop taste by studying great work across all fields — architecture, fashion, music, typography — not just your own industry. Taste cannot be taught from a textbook, but it can be developed through deliberate exposure to excellence and honest assessment of your own work.

## 6. A-Player Discipline

**Principle:** The top 50 people define the company. A-players hire A-players. B-players hire C-players. Once you let a B-player in, the standard drops and never recovers.

**Source:** Jobs spent enormous amounts of time personally recruiting at Apple and NeXT. He interviewed candidates for roles many levels below him. He believed that the difference between a great engineer and an average one was not 2x — it was 50x. A small team of A-players with no process would outperform a large team of B-players with perfect process, every time.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. Would you hire this person again today, knowing what you know now?
2. Is this person in the top 10% of people you have ever worked with at their level?
3. Does this person raise the average quality of the team, or lower it?
4. When A-players on the team hear about this hire, will they be excited or disappointed?
5. Can this person tell you something you do not already know?

**How to Apply:** Never lower the bar because you are in a hurry. A wrong hire costs you the next six right hires who will not want to work alongside mediocrity. Interview for taste, intensity, and the ability to handle ambiguity — not just for skills. After hiring, give A-players the hardest problems and the most autonomy. Protect them from bureaucracy. If someone is not working out, act fast. Carrying B-players is unfair to the A-players who have to compensate for them.

## 7. Product Roadmap Pruning

**Principle:** Say no to 100 good ideas so you can focus on the 1 great one. Innovation is not about saying yes. It is about saying no to almost everything.

**Source:** At Apple's annual strategy offsite, the leadership team would generate a list of the top 10 priorities for the coming year. Then Jobs would cross out the bottom 7. "We can only do three," he would say. The same discipline applied to the iPhone: the team wanted to include dozens of features. Jobs forced them to ship with no copy-paste, no MMS, no third-party apps — because those compromises allowed the things that did ship (multitouch, visual voicemail, mobile Safari) to be perfect.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. If you could only ship three things this year, which three would they be?
2. What are you keeping on the roadmap out of obligation, inertia, or fear rather than conviction?
3. For each initiative: does this move the needle on the one metric that matters most?
4. If you cut this, would any customer actually leave? Or would they not even notice?
5. Is this a "vitamin" (nice to have) or a "painkiller" (must have)?

**How to Apply:** List every initiative on the roadmap. Force-rank them by impact on the customer experience. Draw a line after the top 3. Everything below the line gets killed or deferred indefinitely. Do not keep a "backlog" of nice-to-haves — that is just a guilt list. If an idea is truly great, it will come back. If it does not come back, it was not great. Review the surviving three initiatives weekly. If any of them is not making rapid progress, ask whether it belongs above the line.

## Question Patterns

These are the questions Steve Jobs characteristically asks when evaluating any business decision:

1. **"What are we really trying to do here?"** — Cuts through tactical complexity to find the strategic intent. If you cannot answer this in one sentence, you do not understand your own project.

2. **"Why does this exist?"** — Applied to features, products, teams, and meetings. If the answer is "because we've always done it" or "because the competitor does it," that is not a reason.

3. **"What would we do if we were starting over?"** — Ignores sunk costs entirely. The best answer is often to throw everything away and rebuild from first principles.

4. **"Is this the best we can do?"** — Not "is this good enough" or "does this meet the requirements." Is this the absolute best work this team is capable of? If not, why are we shipping it?

5. **"Who is this for?"** — Forces specificity. "Everyone" is not an answer. "A 28-year-old designer who is frustrated with Photoshop" is an answer.

6. **"What would it take to make this insanely great?"** — Rejects the premise that current constraints are fixed. Explores what would need to change — in timeline, team, technology, or ambition — to achieve something extraordinary.

7. **"Would I want to use this?"** — The ultimate gut check. If the answer is no, everything else is irrelevant.
