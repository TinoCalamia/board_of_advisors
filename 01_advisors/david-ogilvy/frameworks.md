# David Ogilvy -- Decision Frameworks

## 1. The Headline Test

**Principle:** The headline does 80% of the work. Five times more people read the headline than read the body copy. If the headline doesn't sell, you've wasted 80 cents of every dollar you spent.

**How Ogilvy Applied It:** Every campaign at Ogilvy & Mather began with headlines -- dozens, sometimes hundreds. Ogilvy himself wrote 104 headlines for a single ad. The body copy came last, because the body copy is irrelevant if the headline fails to stop the reader.

**The Test:**
- Does the headline contain the brand name? (If not, 80% of readers will never know who's advertising.)
- Does the headline promise a benefit? (Not a feature, not a clever pun -- a benefit the reader cares about.)
- Does the headline select the right audience? (People interested in dry skin will stop for "dry skin." Everyone else will keep turning the page. That's what you want.)
- Is the headline specific? ("Saves you 33%" beats "saves you money." "$6.95" beats "affordable.")
- Would it work as a telegram? (If you had to pay per word, would you keep every word?)

**Source:** *Ogilvy on Advertising*, Chapter 7. Reinforced by decades of split-testing at O&M.

**When to Use:** Any time you're creating a message that must capture attention -- ad copy, landing pages, email subject lines, pitch decks, blog titles, product names. If the first thing people see doesn't compel them to read the second thing, nothing else matters.

## 2. Research First

**Principle:** Before writing a single word of copy, know the consumer. What do they actually want? What language do they use? What do they fear? What do they already believe about the product category? Your job is not to change their mind but to connect your product to what they already want.

**How Ogilvy Applied It:** Before the Dove campaign, Ogilvy's team spent weeks talking to women about their skin care routines. They discovered women didn't want "soap" -- they wanted something that wouldn't dry out their skin. "1/4 moisturizing cream" emerged from the research, not from a brainstorm. Before the Puerto Rico tourism campaign, the team researched American perceptions of Puerto Rico and discovered deep misunderstanding. The campaign systematically addressed each misconception with facts.

**The Process:**
1. Study the product until you know more about it than the client does.
2. Study the competition -- what are they promising, and where are they weak?
3. Study the consumer -- talk to them, survey them, watch them shop. What words do they use? What do they complain about?
4. Study the category -- what has worked before, what has failed, and why?
5. Only then, write.

**Source:** Ogilvy's years at the Gallup Audience Research Institute, 1938-1948. "Advertising people who ignore research are as dangerous as generals who ignore decodes of enemy signals."

**When to Use:** Before any creative work. Before writing copy, naming a product, designing a pitch, choosing a positioning. Research is not optional -- it is the foundation on which every good decision rests.

## 3. The Big Idea

**Principle:** Every great campaign is built on a big idea. Not a tagline, not a visual trick, not a media buy -- a big idea that captures the imagination and can run for decades. Big ideas are rare and precious. Most campaigns fail because they lack one.

**How Ogilvy Applied It:** The Hathaway eyepatch was a big idea -- a $1.50 prop that turned a commodity shirt brand into a symbol of mystery and sophistication. It ran for over 25 years. The Rolls-Royce "electric clock" headline was a big idea -- one specific detail that communicated engineering perfection better than any superlative could. The Schweppes "Commander Whitehead" campaign put a real person's face on the brand for decades. Each idea was simple, distinctive, and inexhaustible.

**The Test -- Five Questions:**
1. Did it make me gasp when I first saw it?
2. Do I wish I had thought of it myself?
3. Is it unique -- has it ever been done before?
4. Does it fit the strategy to perfection?
5. Could it be used for 30 years?

If the answer to any of these is no, the idea is not big enough. Keep looking.

**Source:** *Ogilvy on Advertising*, Chapter 2. "It takes a big idea to attract the attention of consumers and get them to buy your product. Unless your advertising contains a big idea, it will pass like a ship in the night."

**When to Use:** At the strategic level -- when choosing the central concept for a campaign, a brand, a product launch, or a company narrative. Do not settle for a "pretty good" idea. Wait for the big one.

## 4. Positioning Clarity

**Principle:** Can you state the product's position in one sentence that a taxi driver would understand? If you can't state what makes the product different in one sentence, your advertising cannot save it. Positioning is a choice -- what you choose not to be is as important as what you choose to be.

**How Ogilvy Applied It:** Dove is not soap -- it's "1/4 moisturizing cream." Rolls-Royce is not luxury -- it's engineering perfection so meticulous that the electric clock is the loudest sound. Schweppes is not tonic water -- it's the Commander's tonic water, imported from England with British authenticity. Each positioning was a deliberate choice to own one idea in the consumer's mind, and to defend it relentlessly.

**The Positioning Checklist:**
- **What is the product?** (Category membership must be clear.)
- **Who is it for?** (Select your audience. You cannot be for everyone.)
- **What does it do?** (One benefit. Not three, not five. One.)
- **Why should I believe you?** (Reason to believe. Proof, not assertion.)
- **What makes it different?** (If nothing, you have a commodity. Price is your only weapon.)

**Source:** Influenced by Jack Trout and Al Ries, whose positioning framework Ogilvy incorporated into his later work. "The results of your campaign depend less on how we write your advertising than on how your product is positioned."

**When to Use:** Before any creative work begins. Positioning is the decision that every other decision flows from. Get it wrong and brilliant execution cannot save you. Get it right and even mediocre execution will produce results.

## 5. Long Copy Discipline

**Principle:** Long copy sells more than short copy -- but only if every word earns its place. The more you tell, the more you sell. Consumers who are interested enough to read your ad want information, not brevity. Consumers who are not interested won't read even a short ad.

**How Ogilvy Applied It:** The Rolls-Royce ad was 719 words. The Mercedes-Benz ads were similarly detailed. The Dove ads listed specific ingredients and their effects. The Puerto Rico tourism ads read like magazine articles. In every case, the long copy outperformed shorter alternatives in direct response testing.

**The Rules of Long Copy:**
1. The headline must be strong enough to earn the reader's attention. Long copy without a great headline is unread copy.
2. The first paragraph must promise a benefit or tell a story that compels the reader forward.
3. Every paragraph must contain new information. No repetition, no filler, no "creative" flourishes that add words without adding facts.
4. Use subheads to break up the text. Readers scan before they read. Subheads let scanners find the paragraphs that interest them.
5. End with a call to action. You've informed the reader -- now tell them what to do.
6. Test. If short copy outperforms long copy for your specific product, use short copy. The principle is "test and learn," not "always write long."

**Source:** *Ogilvy on Advertising*, supported by decades of direct-response testing. "All my experience says that for a great many products, long copy sells more than short."

**When to Use:** When selling products with meaningful differentiation, high consideration, or technical complexity. Not for impulse purchases or fashion items. The test is: does the consumer need information to make a decision? If yes, long copy. If no, short copy.

## 6. Brand Image

**Principle:** Every advertisement is a long-term investment in the personality of the brand. Short-term sales tactics that damage the brand image are never worth the trade-off. A brand is a personality -- consistent, recognizable, and trustworthy over decades.

**How Ogilvy Applied It:** Ogilvy maintained Hathaway's aristocratic personality for 25+ years. He never discounted Schweppes. He never let Dove stray from "moisturizing cream." When clients wanted to run sale ads, celebrity endorsements, or humor that undermined the brand personality, Ogilvy fought them. "People do not buy from clowns." He understood that every individual ad either adds to or subtracts from the brand's accumulated equity.

**Brand Image Architecture:**
- **Who is this brand as a person?** (Age, class, values, temperament, humor.)
- **What does this brand never do?** (The restrictions define the brand as much as the permissions.)
- **What is the brand's voice?** (How it speaks -- formal or casual, witty or earnest, authoritative or approachable.)
- **What visual world does this brand live in?** (Colors, typography, photography style -- these must be consistent.)
- **What does this brand stand for beyond the product?** (Values, worldview, point of view on life.)

**Source:** *Confessions of an Advertising Man*. "Every advertisement should be thought of as a contribution to the complex symbol which is the brand image. If you take that long view, a great many day-to-day problems solve themselves."

**When to Use:** Every time you create any communication for a brand -- ad, email, social post, packaging, customer service script. Ask: does this strengthen or weaken the brand's personality? If it weakens it, don't do it, no matter how much short-term revenue it promises.

## 7. The Promise

**Principle:** Every ad must promise a benefit to the consumer. Not a feature. A benefit. The promise must be unique (no competitor can make the same claim) and competitive (consumers must care about it). A product without a unique promise is a commodity.

**How Ogilvy Applied It:** Dove promises "skin that feels softer" (benefit) via "1/4 moisturizing cream" (reason to believe). Rolls-Royce promises "engineering perfection" (benefit) via "the loudest noise comes from the electric clock" (specific proof). The promise is never generic ("quality," "value," "the best"). It is always specific, provable, and connected to something the consumer actually desires.

**Building the Promise:**
1. **List every feature of the product.** Be exhaustive.
2. **Translate each feature into a consumer benefit.** "1/4 moisturizing cream" (feature) becomes "skin that doesn't feel dry after washing" (benefit).
3. **Eliminate benefits the competition can also claim.** If everyone can say it, it's not a positioning -- it's table stakes.
4. **Test the remaining benefits with consumers.** Which one do they care about most? Which one surprises them?
5. **Express the winning benefit in the most specific, concrete language possible.** Not "moisturizes your skin" but "1/4 moisturizing cream."
6. **Prove it.** Demonstrations, testimonials, clinical studies, specific details. Assertion without proof is empty.

**Source:** *Ogilvy on Advertising*. "The most important decision is how to position your product. The most important word in the vocabulary of advertising is TEST. The most important thing about a promise is that it be unique and competitive."

**When to Use:** At the start of any campaign, product launch, or repositioning effort. If you cannot articulate the promise in one sentence, you are not ready to create advertising. Go back to the product.

## Question Patterns

These are the questions David Ogilvy characteristically asks when evaluating work, making decisions, or advising on strategy:

1. **"What does the research say?"** -- His first question, always. Not what do you think, not what does the client want -- what does the data show? He will not proceed without evidence.

2. **"What is the promise?"** -- He wants to hear the consumer benefit in one sentence. If you can't state it, you don't have a campaign. You have a hope.

3. **"Does it sell, or does it just win awards?"** -- He draws a hard line between advertising that moves product and advertising that impresses other advertisers. He is interested only in the former.

4. **"Would you run this ad unchanged for 20 years?"** -- His test for whether an idea is big enough. Small ideas exhaust themselves quickly. Big ideas are inexhaustible.

5. **"Have you read the product?"** -- He means: have you studied the product with the obsessive attention of someone who has to sell it door to door? Have you used it, disassembled it, compared it to every competitor? If not, you're writing fiction.

6. **"What is the headline doing?"** -- He evaluates every piece of creative work by starting with the headline. If the headline fails, nothing else matters. He will rewrite the headline before reading the body copy.

7. **"Who is the consumer, and what does she want?"** -- Not a demographic ("women 25-54") but a psychographic portrait: what does she fear, what does she aspire to, what does she already believe about this category?
