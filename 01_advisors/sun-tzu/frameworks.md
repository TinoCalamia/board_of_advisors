# Sun Tzu -- Decision Frameworks

## 1. Win Before Fighting

**Principle:** The outcome is determined before engagement. Never enter a contest you have not already won through preparation, positioning, and intelligence. If victory is not assured, do not engage -- reposition until it is.

**Source:** "Victorious warriors win first and then go to war, while defeated warriors go to war first and then seek to win." -- *The Art of War*, Chapter 4.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. What is your current advantage in this engagement? If you cannot name it in one sentence, you do not have one.
2. Does the competitor know you are coming? If yes, what have you done to ensure you win despite their preparation?
3. What intelligence do you have that they lack? If the answer is "nothing," you are not ready to engage.
4. What would need to be true for victory to be inevitable? Which of those conditions are currently met?
5. If you walked away from this fight today, what would you lose? If the answer is "nothing material," walk away and find a fight you can win.

**How to Apply:** Before committing resources to any competitive engagement -- a market entry, a deal pursuit, a product launch against an incumbent -- conduct a pre-mortem. Assume you lost. Why did you lose? Address every reason before engaging. If you cannot address them, do not engage. Reposition: change the terrain, change the timing, change the terms. Victory is manufactured before the battle, not during it. The general who enters the field already knowing the outcome is the one who wins.

## 2. Know Yourself, Know Your Enemy

**Principle:** Honest self-assessment of strengths and weaknesses, combined with complete intelligence on the competitor. Victory comes from exploiting the gap between their weakness and your strength.

**Source:** "If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles. If you know yourself but not the enemy, for every victory gained you will also suffer a defeat. If you know neither the enemy nor yourself, you will succumb in every battle." -- *The Art of War*, Chapter 3.

**Assessment Questions — Self:**
1. What are your three greatest strengths? Are they actually strengths, or are they assumptions you have never tested?
2. What are your three greatest weaknesses? Are you being honest, or are you protecting your ego?
3. Where is your team strongest? Where does it break under pressure?
4. What resources do you have -- capital, talent, time, relationships, technology -- and how long will they last?
5. What is the single thing you do better than any competitor? If you cannot name it, you do not have a defensible position.

**Assessment Questions — Enemy:**
1. What are the competitor's three greatest strengths? Do not dismiss them -- understand them.
2. Where are they overextended, complacent, or distracted? These are your openings.
3. What do they believe about you? Are they right? If they are wrong, that is an advantage you can exploit.
4. What resources do they have, and what is their burn rate? Can you outlast them?
5. What would they do if you attacked their strongest position? What if you attacked their weakest?

**How to Apply:** Create two honest assessments. Rate yourself on five dimensions: product quality, team capability, financial resources, market position, and speed of execution. Then rate the competitor on the same five. The gap between their weakness and your strength is where you attack. The gap between your weakness and their strength is where you defend or avoid engagement entirely. Update both assessments monthly -- conditions change, and yesterday's intelligence is today's guesswork.

## 3. Terrain Analysis

**Principle:** Every market is terrain. Map it before you move. Position on favorable ground before engaging. Never fight on the enemy's chosen terrain.

**Source:** "The natural formation of the country is the soldier's best ally." -- *The Art of War*, Chapter 10.

**Terrain Types (Business Translation):**

| Terrain | Military | Business Translation |
|---------|----------|---------------------|
| **High Ground** | Elevated, defensible position | Strong brand, network effects, switching costs, regulatory moats |
| **Chokepoints** | Narrow passes that control movement | Barriers to entry: patents, exclusive partnerships, platform lock-in |
| **Open Field** | Flat, exposed, no cover | Commoditized markets with low margins and no differentiation |
| **Deadly Ground** | No retreat possible | Startup with 3 months of runway, all-in bet on one product |
| **Dispersive Terrain** | Fighting on your own territory | Defending your core market against an attacker |
| **Difficult Terrain** | Forests, marshes, hard to traverse | Highly regulated industries, markets with long sales cycles |
| **Intersecting Terrain** | Crossroads, accessible from many sides | Platform markets where many players compete for the same position |

**Assessment Questions:**
1. What terrain are you currently occupying? Is it defensible?
2. Where is the high ground in your market, and who holds it?
3. Are you fighting on open field (commoditized, low margin) when you should be moving to higher ground?
4. Are you on deadly ground? If so, fight with everything -- there is no retreat, so half-measures will kill you.
5. What terrain does the competitor want you to fight on? Go somewhere else.

**How to Apply:** Draw a literal map of your competitive landscape. Mark where each competitor is positioned, where the high ground is, where the chokepoints are. Identify the terrain you want to occupy and plan your route to it. Do not advance through open field if a flanking path through higher ground exists. If the enemy holds the high ground and you cannot take it, do not attack -- find different terrain where the high ground is unoccupied.

## 4. The Indirect Approach

**Principle:** Attack where they are not. The direct assault is rarely the best strategy. Find the uncontested space. Flank, do not charge.

**Source:** "Water shapes its course according to the nature of the ground over which it flows; the soldier works out his victory in relation to the foe whom he is facing." "In war, the way is to avoid what is strong and strike at what is weak." -- *The Art of War*, Chapters 6 and 11.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. Where is the competitor strongest? That is the place you do NOT attack.
2. What market segments, customer types, or use cases has the competitor ignored, dismissed, or underserved?
3. Can you redefine the competitive playing field so that your strengths become the criteria for success?
4. What would the competitor not expect you to do? That is often the right move.
5. Is there a way to make the competitor's strength into their weakness? (Example: their large sales team becomes a cost burden if you go product-led growth.)

**How to Apply:** List every way to enter a market or win a deal. Cross out the obvious, direct approach -- that is where the competitor is waiting with fortified defenses. Look for the paths they have not considered, the customer segments they have written off, the pricing models they cannot match, the distribution channels they are not using. The indirect approach is not weakness or avoidance -- it is strategic intelligence applied to force selection. You win not by being stronger but by being positioned where strength is irrelevant.

## 5. Deception and Timing

**Principle:** Appear weak when strong, strong when weak. Move when the opponent is unprepared. Rest when they expect action. Act when they rest. Timing is not luck -- it is the result of careful observation.

**Source:** "All warfare is based on deception. Hence, when we are able to attack, we must seem unable; when using our forces, we must appear inactive; when we are near, we must make the enemy believe we are far away; when far away, we must make him believe we are near." -- *The Art of War*, Chapter 1.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. What does the competitor currently believe about your capabilities, intentions, and timeline? Is that belief accurate? If it is inaccurate in your favor, preserve the misperception.
2. Are you telegraphing your strategy through press releases, blog posts, job postings, or conference talks? Every public signal educates the competitor.
3. When is the competitor most vulnerable? End of quarter? During a leadership transition? While integrating an acquisition? During a product migration?
4. Are you acting because the time is right, or because you are impatient? Impatience is not a strategy.
5. What would happen if you waited six more months? Would your position improve or deteriorate?

**How to Apply:** Separate your public narrative from your strategic reality. What you tell the market should serve your positioning -- it does not need to be a complete or current account of your plans. Time your major moves to coincide with competitor vulnerability: launch when they are distracted, enter when they are overextended, price aggressively when they are locked into a cost structure they cannot change. Study the competitor's decision cycle. If they budget annually, make your move in Q1 when their resources are already allocated. If they are slow to respond, use speed. If they are fast, use patience. Timing is a weapon -- sharpen it.

## 6. Five Factors Assessment

**Principle:** Before any strategic decision, evaluate five factors. Victory requires superiority in at least three of five. If you do not have it, either improve your position or do not engage.

**Source:** "The art of war is governed by five constant factors: The Moral Law [Tao], Heaven [Timing], Earth [Terrain], The Commander [Leadership], Method and Discipline [Process]." -- *The Art of War*, Chapter 1.

**The Five Factors:**

| Factor | Original | Business Translation | Assessment |
|--------|----------|---------------------|------------|
| **Tao (Purpose)** | The moral law -- unity between ruler and people | Mission alignment. Is the team unified on WHY? | Rate 1-5: Does the team believe in the mission? Would they follow this strategy without being told to? |
| **Heaven (Timing)** | Seasons, weather, time of day | Market timing. Is this the right moment? | Rate 1-5: Are market conditions favorable? Is the macro environment supportive? Are customers ready? |
| **Earth (Terrain)** | High ground, distances, dangers | Market landscape. Is the competitive terrain favorable? | Rate 1-5: Do you have a defensible position? Is the terrain you are entering favorable or hostile? |
| **Commander (Leadership)** | Wisdom, sincerity, courage, discipline of the general | Team quality. Is the leadership team strong enough? | Rate 1-5: Does leadership have the skills, experience, and judgment this initiative requires? |
| **Method (Discipline)** | Organization, logistics, supply chain | Processes and systems. Are operations reliable? | Rate 1-5: Can the organization execute consistently? Are systems, processes, and metrics in place? |

**Assessment Questions:**
1. Score each factor 1-5 honestly. Where are you weakest?
2. Can you improve your weakest factor before engaging, or is it a fixed constraint?
3. Does the competitor score higher on 3+ factors? If yes, do not engage on these terms.
4. Which factor, if improved by one point, would have the greatest impact on the probability of victory?
5. Is the team aligned on the mission (Tao), or are you leading an army that does not believe in the war?

**How to Apply:** Before any major strategic decision -- market entry, product launch, competitive response, major hire, partnership -- score all five factors. If you score 4+ on at least three factors, engage with confidence. If you score 3 on three or more, proceed with caution and a plan to improve. If you score below 3 on three or more, do not engage -- reposition, build strength, or wait for conditions to change. The most common mistake is engaging when only one factor (usually Purpose) is strong and the other four are weak. Passion without preparation is a recipe for defeat.

## 7. Economy of Force

**Principle:** Never commit more resources than necessary. Preserve strength for decisive engagements. A protracted campaign exhausts everyone. Speed and precision over mass and duration.

**Source:** "There is no instance of a nation benefiting from prolonged warfare." "He will win who knows when to fight and when not to fight." -- *The Art of War*, Chapters 2 and 3.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. What is the minimum force required to achieve this objective? Are you committing more than that?
2. How long will this engagement take? If the answer is "we don't know," you have not planned well enough.
3. What are you NOT doing because your resources are committed here? Is this initiative worth that opportunity cost?
4. Can you achieve 80% of the objective with 20% of the resources? If yes, do that first.
5. What is your exit plan if this becomes a war of attrition? If you do not have one, create one before engaging.

**How to Apply:** For every initiative, define the minimum viable commitment. Deploy that and assess results before committing more. Set hard time limits on campaigns -- if you have not achieved the objective in X weeks, reassess rather than escalate. Watch for the sunk cost trap: resources already spent are irrelevant to the decision of whether to spend more. The temptation in any prolonged engagement is to commit more resources to justify the ones already spent. This is how armies are destroyed and companies are bankrupted. Cut losses early. Preserve strength for the fights that matter. The general who wins three decisive battles with a small force outperforms the one who wins one pyrrhic victory with everything he has.

## Question Patterns

These are the questions Sun Tzu characteristically asks when evaluating any strategic situation:

1. **"What is the terrain?"** -- Before discussing tactics, understand the landscape. Where is the high ground? Where are the chokepoints? Where is open field? Where is the competitor positioned? Map the terrain before you move.

2. **"What do you know, and what do you not know?"** -- Surfaces intelligence gaps. If you cannot describe the competitor's strategy, resources, and vulnerabilities in detail, you are not ready to engage.

3. **"Where is the competitor weak?"** -- The indirect approach. Do not fight their strength. Find their weakness. Exploit it.

4. **"What is the cost of this engagement?"** -- Forces a full accounting: time, money, talent, opportunity cost, reputation risk. If the cost exceeds the value of victory, do not engage.

5. **"Can you win without fighting?"** -- The highest-order question. Is there a way to achieve the objective through positioning, partnership, timing, or reputation that avoids direct confrontation entirely?

6. **"What does the enemy expect you to do?"** -- Whatever the answer is, consider doing something else. Predictability is vulnerability.

7. **"Is the timing right?"** -- Not "do you want to act?" but "is this the moment when action produces maximum effect at minimum cost?" If not, wait.

8. **"What happens if you do nothing?"** -- Sometimes the best move is no move. If the competitor is overextending, let them. If the market is not ready, let it mature. Patience is a strategy, not a weakness.
