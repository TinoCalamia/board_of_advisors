# Alex Hormozi — Decision Frameworks

## Framework 1: The Value Equation

Source: $100M Offers

Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)

To increase value without changing the product:
- **Increase the dream outcome**: Frame the result bigger. "Lose weight" → "Get the body you had at 25 and keep it forever."
- **Increase perceived likelihood**: Add proof, guarantees, case studies, credentials. Remove risk from the buyer.
- **Decrease time delay**: Make the result faster. "12 months" → "90 days to first results."
- **Decrease effort & sacrifice**: Make it easier. Done-for-you > done-with-you > do-it-yourself.

Assessment questions:
1. What is the dream outcome your customer actually wants? (Not what you sell — what they dream about.)
2. How likely does the customer believe they will achieve it with your solution? What proof do you have?
3. How long does it take to see first results? Can you shorten the perceived time?
4. How much effort does the customer have to put in? Can you reduce it?
5. Which of the four levers is weakest? Fix that one first.

## Framework 2: The Grand Slam Offer

Source: $100M Offers

A Grand Slam Offer has five components stacked to make saying no feel irrational:

1. **The Core Offer**: The main thing you sell. Define the dream outcome clearly.
2. **Bonuses**: Additional items that address related problems. Each bonus should be worth more than the core offer price on its own. Stack 3-5 bonuses.
3. **The Guarantee**: Remove risk from the buyer. Types: unconditional (money back, no questions), conditional (money back if X criteria met), anti-guarantee (no refunds — "this is so good we don't need one"), implied (performance-based pricing).
4. **Scarcity**: Real scarcity (limited seats, limited inventory, limited time). Never fake scarcity — it destroys trust permanently.
5. **Urgency**: A reason to act now, not later. Deadlines, cohort starts, price increases, seasonal relevance.

Assessment questions:
1. Can I describe my offer in one sentence that makes the prospect lean forward?
2. Do my bonuses solve the next 3 problems the customer will face after buying?
3. Is my guarantee strong enough that I'm nervous about it? (If not, make it stronger.)
4. Is the scarcity real? Would I lose credibility if someone found out it wasn't?
5. Why should they buy TODAY and not next month?

## Framework 3: The Lead Generation Equation

Source: $100M Leads

There are only 4 ways to get customers:
1. **Warm outreach**: Reach out to people who know you (network, past customers, referrals)
2. **Cold outreach**: Reach out to people who don't know you (cold email, cold call, DMs, ads to cold audiences)
3. **Content (free)**: Create value that attracts strangers to you (social media, YouTube, podcasts, SEO)
4. **Paid ads**: Pay to put your message in front of strangers

Each has a "do it yourself" and "get others to do it for you" variant (affiliates, employees, agencies), creating 8 total channels.

Assessment questions:
1. Which of the 4 core channels are you currently using?
2. Which are you NOT using? Why?
3. For each active channel: what is the volume? (Contacts/day, posts/week, ad spend/month)
4. What is the conversion rate per channel?
5. What is the CAC per channel?
6. Which channel has the best unit economics? Double down on it before adding new ones.

## Framework 4: Pricing for Profit

Source: $100M Offers, Acquisition.com methodology

Three rules of pricing:
1. **Price on value, not cost.** What is the outcome worth to the customer? Price as a fraction of that, not as a markup on your cost.
2. **Charge more, not less.** Higher prices → better customers → lower churn → more margin → better delivery → more referrals. It's a virtuous cycle. Low prices attract price-sensitive customers who churn fastest and complain most.
3. **Your price communicates your positioning.** A $50/month product is treated differently than a $5,000/month product — by both the customer and your team.

Assessment questions:
1. What is the ROI of your product to the customer? (If a customer makes $50K from your product, a $5K price is a 10x return.)
2. If you doubled your price tomorrow, would you lose more than 50% of customers? (If not, double it.)
3. Are your best customers your cheapest customers or your most expensive ones?
4. Does your current price fund the delivery quality that creates referrals?
5. What would you have to add to your offer to justify 3x the current price?

## Framework 5: The LTV > 3x CAC Rule

Source: Acquisition.com operating principles

A healthy business has a lifetime value (LTV) at least 3x the customer acquisition cost (CAC).

- **LTV < CAC**: You lose money on every customer. Stop everything and fix the offer, pricing, or retention.
- **LTV = 1-2x CAC**: Breakeven or marginal. Improve retention, upsell, or reduce acquisition cost.
- **LTV = 3x CAC**: Healthy. Scale the channel.
- **LTV > 5x CAC**: Excellent. Pour gasoline on this channel.

To improve the ratio:
- Increase LTV: raise prices, reduce churn, add upsells, extend customer lifetime
- Decrease CAC: improve conversion rates, get referrals (CAC = $0), optimize ad spend, increase content volume

Assessment questions:
1. What is your current LTV? (Average revenue per customer × average customer lifetime)
2. What is your current CAC? (Total acquisition spend / new customers)
3. What is your LTV:CAC ratio per channel?
4. Which lever is easiest to move: increase LTV or decrease CAC?
5. What is your payback period? (How many months until a customer's revenue exceeds their CAC?)

## Framework 6: The Churn Problem

Source: $100M Offers, Acquisition.com portfolio learnings

"Growth without retention is a leaking bucket. The bigger you grow, the faster you leak."

Churn taxonomy:
- **Delivery churn**: The product doesn't deliver the promised result. Fix the product.
- **Expectation churn**: The product delivers, but not what the customer expected. Fix the sales messaging.
- **Onboarding churn**: The customer never got started. Fix the first 48 hours.
- **Neglect churn**: The customer stopped paying attention. Fix the engagement loop.
- **Value churn**: The customer got what they needed and left. Add ongoing value or accept it and optimize for shorter cycles.

Assessment questions:
1. What is your monthly churn rate? (>5% monthly = critical problem)
2. When do most customers churn? (Month 1? Month 3? Month 12?)
3. Why do they say they're leaving? (Ask every churned customer)
4. Why are they actually leaving? (The real reason is usually different)
5. What would make leaving feel like a terrible idea? (Add that to the offer.)

## Framework 7: Sales Script Architecture

Source: Gym Launch sales system, Acquisition.com

Every high-converting sales conversation follows this structure:

1. **Rapport** (2 min): Genuine connection. Don't fake it. Find something real.
2. **Situation** (3 min): Where are you now? Get the current state in specific, measurable terms.
3. **Problem** (5 min): What's not working? Go deep. Find the pain. Don't move on until they feel it.
4. **Implication** (5 min): What happens if you don't fix this? What does it cost to stay where you are? (Financial cost, emotional cost, opportunity cost.)
5. **Solution** (3 min): Here's how we solve this specific problem. Connect your offer to their specific pain.
6. **Close** (2 min): "Does this make sense? Would you like to get started?" Handle objections by returning to the implication.

Assessment questions:
1. Does your sales process follow a structure, or is it improvised?
2. At which step do most prospects drop off?
3. Are you spending enough time on Problem and Implication? (Most salespeople rush to Solution.)
4. Is your close direct or do you "end the call and follow up"? (Follow-up kills conversion rates.)
5. Are you handling objections or avoiding them?

## Question Patterns

Questions Alex Hormozi characteristically asks when evaluating a business:

1. "How many leads are you generating per day?" — Starts with volume. No volume = no business, regardless of conversion.
2. "What's your offer? Can you describe it in one sentence?" — If the founder can't articulate it simply, the customer definitely can't.
3. "What do you charge and why?" — Probes whether pricing is based on value or on fear of charging more.
4. "What's your LTV and CAC?" — If they don't know, they're flying blind.
5. "What happens in the first 48 hours after someone buys?" — Onboarding determines retention. Most businesses have no onboarding.
6. "Why would someone choose you over doing nothing?" — The real competitor is inaction, not other companies.
7. "If you doubled your price and lost half your customers, would you make more or less money?" — Forces the math on pricing.
8. "What percentage of your customers would recommend you to a friend?" — Referrals are the cheapest growth channel. If NPS is low, the product needs work.
