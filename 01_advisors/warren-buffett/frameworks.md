# Warren Buffett — Decision Frameworks

## 1. Moat Analysis

**Principle:** A great business has a durable competitive advantage — a moat — that protects it from competitors the way a castle moat protects against invaders. The wider the moat, the more valuable the business. The key question is not "does this business have a moat?" but "is the moat getting wider or narrower?"

**Source:** Buffett has used the moat metaphor since the 1990s to describe businesses like Coca-Cola (brand), GEICO (cost advantage), Apple (ecosystem switching costs), and American Express (network effects). He looks for businesses whose moats are structural, not dependent on a single product or person.

**Five Forms of Economic Moats:**

**Network Effects** — The product becomes more valuable as more people use it. Each new user increases the value for all existing users.
- How many users or participants does this network have?
- What happens to the experience when one more person joins?
- Could a competitor replicate this network from scratch? How long would it take?

**Switching Costs** — Customers are locked in because the cost of switching (time, money, data migration, retraining) exceeds the benefit.
- If a competitor offered the same product at 20% less, would customers actually switch?
- What would a customer lose by leaving — data, integrations, workflows, relationships?
- Are switching costs increasing or decreasing over time?

**Cost Advantages** — The company can deliver the same product at lower cost due to scale, process, geography, or technology.
- What is the unit cost relative to the next best competitor?
- Is this cost advantage structural (scale, patents) or operational (better management)?
- Can a well-funded competitor replicate this cost structure within 5 years?

**Intangible Assets** — Brands, patents, regulatory licenses, or proprietary data that competitors cannot easily replicate.
- Can this business raise prices by 10% without losing customers? If yes, the brand has real pricing power.
- Are the patents or licenses expiring? What happens when they do?
- Is the brand strengthening or weakening with younger customers?

**Efficient Scale** — The market is only big enough to support one or two profitable players, so new entrants cannot justify the investment.
- How large is the addressable market relative to the minimum efficient scale?
- What would it cost a new entrant to reach profitability? Is that investment justified by the available returns?
- Are there regulatory or geographic barriers that limit the number of players?

**How to Apply:** For every business you evaluate, identify which moat type(s) apply and assess whether they are widening or narrowing. A business with a narrowing moat is a sale, regardless of current financials. A business with a widening moat is a hold forever, regardless of current price volatility.

## 2. Circle of Competence

**Principle:** Operate only within what you truly understand. The size of the circle matters far less than knowing exactly where the boundary is. Disaster comes not from having a small circle but from thinking your circle is larger than it actually is.

**Source:** Buffett refused to invest in technology companies for decades — not because he thought they were bad businesses, but because he could not predict their economics 20 years out. He watched friends make billions in the dot-com boom without flinching. When he finally invested in Apple in 2016, it was not as a technology bet — it was a consumer brand bet, which was squarely inside his circle. He understood brand loyalty, pricing power, and ecosystem lock-in. He did not need to understand semiconductor engineering.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. Can you explain this business's economics to a smart 12-year-old? If not, it is outside your circle.
2. Can you predict with reasonable confidence what this business will look like in 10 years? If not, you are guessing, not investing.
3. Have you spent at least 100 hours studying this industry? Reading about it in the news does not count. Annual reports, competitor analysis, and unit economics count.
4. Could you write a one-page memo explaining why this business will be more valuable in a decade — without referencing market trends, momentum, or what other investors are doing?
5. When you hear experts discuss this field, do you understand their reasoning, or are you just trusting their conclusions?

**How to Apply:** Before any major decision — investment, partnership, market entry, product launch — honestly assess whether it falls inside your circle of competence. If it does not, you have two options: expand the circle through deep study (which takes months or years, not days), or decline the opportunity and wait for one that falls inside your circle. The second option is almost always the right one. The opportunity cost of passing on a good deal outside your circle is always less than the cost of making a bad decision inside someone else's circle.

## 3. Margin of Safety

**Principle:** Never pay full price. Always leave room for error. The future is inherently uncertain. Your analysis will be wrong sometimes. The margin of safety ensures that when you are wrong, you survive.

**Source:** Ben Graham's foundational concept, taught to Buffett at Columbia. Graham compared it to engineering a bridge: if the maximum load is 10,000 pounds, you build it to handle 30,000. Berkshire Hathaway's insurance float — billions in cash available for deployment — is itself a margin of safety. Buffett keeps far more cash on hand than any analyst recommends, because cash is the ability to act when others cannot.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. What is the worst-case scenario, and can you survive it? Not "is the worst case likely" — can you survive it if it happens?
2. What are you paying relative to the intrinsic value? If the margin is less than 25%, the deal is not compelling enough.
3. How much debt is involved? Leverage eliminates your margin of safety. It turns small mistakes into fatal ones.
4. If your most optimistic assumption turns out to be wrong, does the deal still work?
5. Do you have reserves — cash, time, optionality — to weather an extended period of adversity?

**How to Apply:** Calculate the intrinsic value of the opportunity conservatively. Then demand a significant discount — at least 25-30% below your conservative estimate. If the opportunity does not offer that discount, wait. There will always be another pitch. This applies beyond investing: when estimating project timelines, add 50%. When budgeting, hold 30% in reserve. When forecasting revenue, use the pessimistic scenario as your base case. Build slack into every system.

## 4. Owner Earnings

**Principle:** Ignore accounting earnings. GAAP earnings are distorted by depreciation schedules, amortization of goodwill, one-time charges, and creative accounting. The number that matters is what the owner actually takes home after maintaining the business in its current competitive position.

**Source:** Buffett introduced "owner earnings" in his 1986 shareholder letter as the correct way to evaluate a business. It is defined as: reported earnings + depreciation/amortization - average annual capital expenditure required to maintain competitive position. This is the true cash flow available to the owner. Buffett used this framework to evaluate See's Candies (high owner earnings relative to purchase price), newspapers (declining owner earnings as competitive position weakened), and insurance businesses (where float amplifies owner earnings).

**Assessment Questions:**
1. What are the reported earnings, and how much of that is real cash versus accounting adjustments?
2. What is the maintenance capex — not growth capex, maintenance capex — required to keep the business in its current competitive position?
3. What are owner earnings as a percentage of revenue? Is that percentage growing or shrinking?
4. How much of the reported earnings could the owner actually withdraw without damaging the business?
5. Are there hidden liabilities — pension obligations, deferred maintenance, legal exposure — that reduce real owner earnings below what the financials show?

**How to Apply:** Take the reported net income. Add back depreciation and amortization. Subtract the capital expenditure needed to maintain (not grow) the current business. The result is owner earnings. Divide the purchase price or market cap by owner earnings to get the owner earnings multiple. Compare this multiple to a risk-free rate of return (e.g., a 10-year government bond). If the owner earnings yield (inverse of the multiple) significantly exceeds the risk-free rate, the investment may be attractive. If it does not, you are not being paid enough for the risk.

## 5. Inversion (via Charlie Munger)

**Principle:** Instead of asking how to succeed, ask how to fail — then systematically avoid every path to failure. "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."

**Source:** Charlie Munger, Buffett's partner, borrowed this from the mathematician Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi, who said "Invert, always invert" as a problem-solving technique. Munger applied it to business and life decisions. Buffett adopted it as a core analytical method. When evaluating Berkshire acquisitions, Buffett asks not "what could go right?" but "what could go wrong, and how badly?"

**Assessment Questions:**
1. If you wanted this project to fail spectacularly, what would you do? Now check — are you doing any of those things?
2. What are the three most likely ways this business or initiative dies? What are you doing to prevent each one?
3. What assumption, if proven wrong, would make this entire plan worthless? How confident are you in that assumption?
4. What happened to the last three companies that tried this? Why did they fail? Are you doing anything differently?
5. If you told a smart skeptic about this plan, what would their strongest objection be? Can you answer it convincingly?

**How to Apply:** Before committing to any decision, write a pre-mortem. Imagine it is one year from now and the decision has failed catastrophically. Write down every reason it failed. Now go back to the present and evaluate whether any of those failure modes are already present or likely. Address each one before proceeding. This is not pessimism — it is risk management. The goal is not to avoid all risk but to avoid the risks you could have foreseen and prevented.

## 6. The Newspaper Test

**Principle:** Would you be comfortable if your decision were reported on the front page of your local newspaper tomorrow, written by a smart, critical reporter and read by your family, friends, and community? If not, do not make the decision.

**Source:** Buffett has used this test as Berkshire Hathaway's ethical framework for decades. He tells his managers: "I want you to ask yourself whether you are willing to have any contemplated act appear the next day on the front page of your local paper — to be read by your spouse, your children, and your friends — with the reporting done by an informed and critical reporter." This is not about legality. Many legal actions fail the newspaper test. It is about whether you would be proud of the decision in full public view.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. If a journalist investigated this decision and published every detail, would you be comfortable with the story?
2. Would you explain this decision the same way to your biggest customer, your most important employee, and a regulator?
3. Is there any aspect of this decision that relies on secrecy to be acceptable? If so, that aspect is a problem.
4. If your competitors made this exact decision, would you criticize them for it?
5. Twenty years from now, will you be proud of this decision or will you wish you could take it back?

**How to Apply:** Before any significant decision — a partnership, a pricing change, an employee termination, a marketing claim — run the newspaper test. If any part of the decision makes you uncomfortable under scrutiny, rethink it. This does not mean you avoid all hard decisions. Hard decisions — layoffs, price increases, ending partnerships — can pass the newspaper test if the reasoning is sound and the process is fair. The test catches decisions where the reasoning is self-serving, the process is dishonest, or the impact is disproportionately harmful to people who have no voice.

## 7. The 20-Slot Punch Card

**Principle:** Imagine you receive a punch card at birth with only 20 slots. Every time you make a major decision — an investment, a business commitment, a strategic bet — one slot gets punched. When all 20 slots are used, you are done for life. This forces quality over quantity, patience over action, and conviction over diversification.

**Source:** Buffett has used this thought experiment in shareholder meetings and interviews for decades. He argues that most investors would be better off if they were limited to 20 investments in their entire lifetime, because the constraint would force them to think deeply, wait patiently, and act only with extreme conviction. The punch card is a mental model for resource allocation: your time, attention, and capital are finite. Treat them that way.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. Is this opportunity compelling enough that you would use one of your 20 lifetime slots on it?
2. Can you articulate in two sentences why this is a once-in-a-decade opportunity? If it takes a paragraph, it is not compelling enough.
3. If you had to hold this position or commitment for 20 years with no ability to exit, would you still proceed?
4. How does this compare to the best opportunity you have ever seen? Is it in the same league?
5. What is the opportunity cost? By punching this slot, what future opportunity might you have to decline?

**How to Apply:** Keep a mental (or literal) punch card. Before any major commitment — hiring a key executive, entering a new market, making a significant investment, signing a major partnership — ask whether this merits one of your 20 slots. If the answer is not an emphatic yes, the answer is no. This framework eliminates the 80% of decisions that are "pretty good" but not "extraordinary." It is the antidote to FOMO, to diversification for its own sake, and to the restless urge to always be doing something. The greatest returns in life come from a very small number of decisions made with very high conviction.

## Question Patterns

These are the questions Warren Buffett characteristically asks when evaluating any business decision:

1. **"What are the unit economics?"** — Strips away narrative, brand, and hype to focus on the fundamental math. How much does it cost to acquire a customer, serve them, and retain them? What is the lifetime value? If the unit economics do not work, nothing else matters.

2. **"What is the moat, and is it getting wider or narrower?"** — Identifies the competitive advantage and, more importantly, its trajectory. A widening moat justifies a premium. A narrowing moat is a warning sign, regardless of current financials.

3. **"What would I do if I could not sell this for ten years?"** — Eliminates speculation and short-term thinking. Forces you to evaluate the underlying business quality rather than the potential for a quick flip.

4. **"How could this kill us?"** — Inversion applied directly. Identifies existential risks before they materialize. A business that cannot answer this question clearly has not thought hard enough.

5. **"Does this business have pricing power?"** — The single most important question in business economics. If you can raise prices without losing customers, you have a great business. If you have to hold a prayer meeting before raising prices, you do not.

6. **"Who is the manager, and would I trust them with my daughter's inheritance?"** — Character matters more than credentials. Buffett looks for managers who are honest, passionate about the business, and think like owners rather than employees.

7. **"Am I being greedy when I should be fearful, or fearful when I should be greedy?"** — A real-time emotional check. The best opportunities feel uncomfortable because they require acting against the crowd. If a decision feels easy and popular, ask what you are missing.
