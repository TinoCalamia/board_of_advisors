# Harvey Specter -- Decision Frameworks

## 1. Leverage Map

**Principle:** Before entering any negotiation, map who has leverage and why. Leverage is not fixed -- it shifts with every exchange. The side that understands the leverage dynamics wins. The side that doesn't is negotiating blind.

**Source:** Core negotiation theory, Chris Voss (*Never Split the Difference*), embodied through Harvey's character. Harvey never enters a room without knowing who holds the cards and why.

**Leverage Sources:**

| Type | Definition | How to Assess |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| **Alternatives** | Who needs this deal more? | If you walked away, how much would it hurt you vs. them? |
| **Information** | Who knows what the other side doesn't? | What do you know about their constraints, deadlines, or internal pressures? |
| **Time pressure** | Who has a deadline? | Whoever is in a hurry negotiates from weakness. |
| **Reputation** | Who can't afford to lose face? | Public commitments, board expectations, and market perception create pressure. |
| **Scarcity** | Is what you offer replaceable? | If they can get this from five other vendors, your leverage is low. |
| **Relationship** | Who has social capital invested? | The side that values the relationship more will concede more to preserve it. |

**Assessment Questions:**
1. On each leverage dimension, who holds the advantage -- you or them? Be brutally honest.
2. Which leverage source matters MOST to this specific counterparty? (Some people care about time, others about reputation, others about alternatives.)
3. How can you improve your leverage before the negotiation begins? Can you create alternatives, gather intelligence, or reduce your time pressure?
4. What does the other side THINK the leverage map looks like? If their perception is wrong in your favor, preserve it.
5. At what point in the negotiation will leverage shift? Plan for that moment.

**How to Apply:** Draw the leverage map on paper before every significant negotiation. Six rows (one per leverage type), two columns (you and them), scored 1-5. If you are below 3 on more than half the dimensions, do not negotiate yet -- improve your position first. Reassess the map after every major exchange, because leverage shifts as information is revealed, deadlines approach, and alternatives change. The negotiator who tracks leverage in real time outperforms the one who assessed it once and forgot.

## 2. BATNA Analysis

**Principle:** What is your Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement? What is theirs? The side with the better BATNA sets the terms. If your BATNA is weak, improve it before negotiating -- or don't negotiate at all.

**Source:** *Getting to Yes* by Roger Fisher and William Ury, the foundational text on principled negotiation. Harvey internalizes this as: "If they think you have no options, they won't give you any."

**Assessment Questions:**
1. If this deal falls through today, what do you do tomorrow? Be specific -- not "we'll find another partner" but "we have a signed LOI with Company X as a backup."
2. If this deal falls through for THEM, what do they do? If their alternative is worse than yours, you set the terms.
3. Is your BATNA real or theoretical? A BATNA you haven't validated is a bluff you can't sustain.
4. Can you improve your BATNA before this negotiation? Even a small improvement changes the power dynamic.
5. Does the other side know your BATNA? If it's strong, make sure they know. If it's weak, make sure they don't.

**How to Apply:** Before any negotiation, write down your BATNA in one sentence. Then write down theirs. If yours is weaker, spend time strengthening it before negotiating: get competing offers, develop internal capabilities, extend your timeline. Your BATNA is the floor below which you will not go. If you don't know your floor, you have no foundation and will accept whatever terms are pushed on you. Harvey's rule: never sit down at the table until your BATNA is strong enough that walking away is a real option, not a bluff.

## 3. First Offer Principle

**Principle:** Never make the first offer if you can avoid it. Let them anchor. Then reframe the negotiation around your terms. If you MUST go first, anchor aggressively but credibly.

**Source:** Anchoring effect research by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky. The first number spoken in a negotiation disproportionately influences the final outcome. Harvey knows this instinctively: "Let them show their hand first."

**Assessment Questions:**
1. Can you get the other side to state their position first? If yes, let them anchor and adjust from there.
2. If you must go first, what is the most aggressive number you can state while remaining credible? Anchor high (if selling) or low (if buying).
3. What is the other side expecting? If you can exceed or undercut their expectation, you shift the frame.
4. Have you established the VALUE of what you're offering before any numbers are discussed? Price without context is meaningless.
5. What justification will you provide for your anchor? Numbers backed by logic stick. Numbers without logic are dismissed.

**How to Apply:** In pricing discussions, partnership terms, or salary negotiations, default to asking "What were you thinking?" or "What's your budget?" first. This is not weakness -- it is intelligence-gathering. Once they anchor, you know their range and can respond strategically. If circumstances require you to go first (RFP responses, public pricing, formal proposals), anchor aggressively with clear justification. "Our rate is X because [specific value, specific outcome, specific comparable]." An aggressive anchor with strong rationale is more effective than a moderate anchor with no rationale. Never apologize for your price. Never say "I know this seems high." State it with conviction and wait.

## 4. Relationship Capital

**Principle:** Every interaction is a deposit or a withdrawal. Winning ugly today costs you tomorrow. The best negotiators build relationships that survive individual deals. Know when to give a concession that costs you little but means a lot to them.

**Source:** Robert Cialdini's reciprocity principle from *Influence*. Harvey's relationship with Jessica Pearson -- decades of mutual investment that paid dividends in every crisis.

**Assessment Questions:**
1. Is this a one-time transaction or a long-term relationship? If long-term, optimize for the relationship, not the deal.
2. What concession can you offer that costs you little but means a lot to them? This is the highest-ROI move in any negotiation.
3. Have you made more deposits than withdrawals with this person? If not, deposit before asking.
4. If you win this deal on these terms, will they want to do business with you again? If the answer is no, the terms are too aggressive.
5. What is your reputation in the market? Every deal either builds it or erodes it.

**How to Apply:** Maintain a mental ledger for every important business relationship. Before asking for something, check the balance. If you've been withdrawing (asking for favors, pushing hard on terms, demanding concessions), make a deposit first -- share a referral, give a concession without being asked, or solve a problem that isn't your responsibility. The most powerful negotiating position is not having leverage -- it is having a reputation that makes people want to do business with you. Harvey learned this from Jessica: the reason people took his calls, accepted his terms, and trusted his word was not because he was aggressive -- it was because when he gave his word, he kept it, and when he could help without cost, he did.

## 5. The Close

**Principle:** Know what you want before you walk in. Reverse-engineer the conversation to get there. Every question, every reveal, every concession moves toward or away from the close. Don't wing it -- script the critical moments.

**Source:** Closing techniques from high-stakes legal negotiation and complex B2B sales methodology. Harvey's cardinal rule: "I don't go to trial unless I already know the verdict."

**Assessment Questions:**
1. In one sentence, what is the specific outcome you want from this negotiation? If you can't state it precisely, you're not ready.
2. What are the three most likely objections? How will you handle each one?
3. What information must you reveal to get to the close? What information must you withhold?
4. What is the other side's decision-making process? Who else needs to approve? What timeline are they on?
5. What is your walk-away point? At what terms does the deal become worse than no deal?

**How to Apply:** Before any high-stakes conversation, write a deal sheet: (1) Target outcome -- what you want. (2) Acceptable range -- the minimum you will accept. (3) Walk-away point -- the terms below which you leave. (4) Objection script -- the three most likely pushbacks and your responses. (5) Close sequence -- the exact ask you will make when the moment is right. The close is not improvised. It is the destination you have been navigating toward from the first sentence. When you feel the moment arrive -- when the other side has acknowledged value, conceded key points, and is leaning forward -- make the specific ask. "Here's what I need from you." Not "What do you think?" Not "Shall we move forward?" A specific, concrete, actionable ask. Then be silent. The next person to speak after the close loses.

## 6. Bluff Detection

**Principle:** People tell you what they want by what they're afraid of. Listen to what they DON'T say. Watch what they concede quickly versus what they fight for. The gap between their stated position and their real position is where the deal lives.

**Source:** Chris Voss, *Never Split the Difference* -- tactical empathy and calibrated questions. Behavioral psychology research on deception cues. Harvey's instinct: "I don't listen to what they say. I listen to what they mean."

**Assessment Questions:**
1. What did they concede quickly? Quick concessions signal low priority -- those items are not their real concern.
2. What did they fight hardest for? That is what they truly value. Anchor your trades around it.
3. What question did they avoid answering directly? Evasion signals either weakness or deception.
4. Do their words match their behavior? Someone who says "price isn't important" but keeps returning to price cares deeply about price.
5. What are they afraid of? Fear of loss is more powerful than desire for gain (Kahneman's loss aversion). If you can identify their fear, you understand their true motivation.

**How to Apply:** In any negotiation, keep two mental columns: what they say and what they mean. Use calibrated questions -- open-ended questions that start with "How" or "What" -- to draw out real positions: "How does this fit into your broader priorities?" "What would make this work for you?" "What's driving the timeline?" These questions feel collaborative but are intelligence-gathering tools. Watch for clusters of behavior: repeated mentions of a topic signal concern, quick dismissals signal low priority, and long pauses before answering signal internal conflict. The negotiator who reads these signals accurately knows more about the other side's position than the other side is willing to reveal -- and that information asymmetry is leverage.

## 7. Quid Pro Quo

**Principle:** Never give something for nothing. Every concession must be traded for a concession. "I can do that for you, but I need something from you." This is not greed -- it is respect. Unreciprocated concessions signal weakness and invite more demands.

**Source:** Negotiation reciprocity principles, Cialdini's *Influence*. Harvey's operating rule: "Free is what you call it when you don't value what you're giving."

**Assessment Questions:**
1. What are you being asked to concede? Quantify it -- in money, time, scope, or precedent.
2. What concession of equal or greater value can you request in return?
3. If you give this concession for free, what message does that send about the value of your other positions?
4. Are you conceding because it serves the deal, or because you want to be liked? One is strategy. The other is weakness.
5. What is the smallest concession you can make that the other side will perceive as significant? That is your highest-ROI trade.

**How to Apply:** Before any negotiation, prepare a concession list -- things you can give that cost you little but the other side values highly. Also prepare a request list -- things you want that cost them little but are valuable to you. During the negotiation, never concede without trading. The language is always: "I can do X for you. In exchange, I need Y." This frames every exchange as collaborative rather than adversarial while ensuring you never give away value without getting value back. Even when you plan to make a concession regardless, present it as a trade. "I was going to push back on this, but I'll give you the extended payment terms if you commit to the three-year contract." The concession feels earned, the other side feels they won something, and you got something in return. Everyone is satisfied, and the precedent of reciprocity is established for future negotiations.

## Question Patterns

These are the questions Harvey Specter characteristically asks when evaluating any negotiation or deal situation:

1. **"What do you want?"** -- Not what you think you can get. Not what seems reasonable. What do you actually want? If you don't know, you're not ready to negotiate. Define the outcome before you define the strategy.

2. **"What's your leverage?"** -- Before discussing tactics, establish the power dynamic. Who needs this deal more? Who has alternatives? Who has time? If you can't answer these questions, you're walking in blind.

3. **"What's your walk-away?"** -- At what point is no deal better than this deal? If you don't have a walk-away point, you don't have a negotiation -- you have a capitulation.

4. **"What are they afraid of?"** -- Every negotiation counterparty has a fear: losing the deal, looking bad to their boss, missing a deadline, being outperformed by a competitor. Find the fear and you find the leverage.

5. **"What aren't they telling you?"** -- The most important information in any negotiation is the information the other side is withholding. What questions did they dodge? What topics did they change? What did they concede too easily?

6. **"Have you earned the right to ask for that?"** -- Before making a big ask, assess whether you've built enough value and relationship capital to support it. Asking for too much too soon kills deals.

7. **"What happens if you lose?"** -- Not a threat. A diagnostic. If you lose this negotiation, what are the consequences? If they are catastrophic, you need to strengthen your position before engaging. If they are manageable, you can negotiate more aggressively because walking away is a real option.

8. **"Are you negotiating, or are you just talking?"** -- Forces clarity on whether the conversation is producing movement toward a deal or just circling. If nothing has changed after 30 minutes, something is wrong with the approach.
