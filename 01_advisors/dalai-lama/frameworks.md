# Dalai Lama Decision Frameworks

## 1. Interdependence Test

**Principle:** No action exists in isolation. Who else is affected? Trace the ripple effects outward.

**How it works:**

Every decision radiates consequences beyond its intended target. A pricing change affects not only revenue but also which customers can afford the product, which employees feel proud of what they sell, which competitors respond and how, and what precedent is set for future decisions. The Western business mind tends to draw a tight circle around a decision and optimize within that circle. The interdependence test demands a wider circle.

Trace the ripple effects in concentric rings:
- **Ring 1 — Direct stakeholders:** Who is immediately affected? Employees, customers, partners.
- **Ring 2 — Extended stakeholders:** Their families, their communities, their other relationships.
- **Ring 3 — Systemic effects:** The industry, the market, the cultural norms you are reinforcing or undermining.
- **Ring 4 — Future generations:** What precedent does this set? What world does this decision help create?

A decision that helps the company but harms the ecosystem will eventually harm the company, because the company exists within the ecosystem. A factory that pollutes the river it draws water from is destroying itself slowly. Most harmful business decisions follow this pattern — they extract value from the system they depend on.

**Application steps:**
1. Name the decision.
2. List everyone affected in each ring (be honest — include the people you would rather not think about).
3. For each group, ask: does this decision increase or decrease their well-being?
4. If the decision harms Ring 2, 3, or 4 to benefit Ring 1, ask: is there an alternative that serves Ring 1 without creating harm further out?
5. If no such alternative exists, ask: is the harm proportionate and unavoidable, or is it a cost you are externalizing because it is convenient?

**Source:** Buddhist concept of dependent origination (pratityasamutpada) — the teaching that all phenomena arise in dependence on causes and conditions. Nothing exists independently. Applied practically, this means every business decision is an intervention in a web of relationships, not an isolated event.

## 2. Compassion Audit

**Principle:** What is the real motivation behind this decision? Examine the inner motivation honestly. Decisions born from compassion create sustainable outcomes. Decisions born from fear create more fear.

**How it works:**

Before evaluating the strategic merits of a decision, examine its emotional root. Most decisions are motivated by one of a handful of inner states:
- **Fear:** "If we don't do this, we'll fall behind / lose market share / go bankrupt."
- **Greed:** "This will maximize our revenue / valuation / competitive advantage."
- **Ego:** "This will prove that we're the best / that the critics were wrong / that I was right."
- **Genuine concern:** "This will help our customers / team / community in a meaningful way."

None of these motivations is inherently wrong. Fear sometimes contains accurate information. Ambition drives progress. But the motivation shapes the execution. A product built from fear of competition tends to be reactive and derivative. A product built from genuine desire to solve a problem tends to be original and durable. The same decision can be made from different motivations and produce radically different outcomes.

**Application steps:**
1. State the decision you are about to make.
2. Complete this sentence honestly: "The real reason I want to do this is..."
3. If the answer involves fear, ask: "What specifically am I afraid of? Is the fear proportionate to the actual risk?"
4. If the answer involves ego, ask: "Would I still make this decision if nobody would ever know it was mine?"
5. If the answer involves greed, ask: "Am I optimizing for maximum extraction or maximum creation of value?"
6. If the answer involves genuine concern, ask: "Am I sure this is genuine, or am I using altruistic language to disguise a less noble motivation?"

**The litmus test:** Imagine explaining this decision — including the real motivation behind it — to someone you deeply respect. Are you comfortable? If not, the motivation needs examination.

**Source:** Core Buddhist teaching on the three poisons (greed, hatred, delusion) and their antidotes (generosity, compassion, wisdom). The Dalai Lama applies this framework not as a moral judgment but as a practical diagnostic: understanding your true motivation gives you better information about the likely quality of your decision.

## 3. Long-term vs Short-term

**Principle:** Will this matter in 10 years? In 100 years? Most business urgency is artificial.

**How it works:**

The Dalai Lama has led a cause for over 70 years. He has watched empires rise and fall, ideologies gain and lose dominance, technologies transform and become obsolete. From this vantage point, most of what feels urgent in business is ephemeral.

This framework distinguishes between three categories of urgency:
- **Truly urgent:** People are suffering now. An existential threat is materializing. A moral line is being crossed. These demand immediate action.
- **Artificially urgent:** Quarterly targets, competitor launches, board pressure, news cycles, FOMO. These feel urgent because of external pressure, not because the underlying reality demands immediate action.
- **Important but not urgent:** Culture building, relationship deepening, skill development, purpose clarification. These are perpetually pushed aside by the artificially urgent, even though they determine long-term outcomes more than anything in the second category.

**Application steps:**
1. When facing a "urgent" decision, ask: "Will this matter in 10 years?"
2. If yes, it deserves deep consideration — do not rush it just because it also feels urgent.
3. If no, it is probably artificially urgent. Handle it efficiently but do not give it the emotional weight of a truly consequential decision.
4. Ask: "What important-but-not-urgent work am I neglecting because of this urgency?" That neglected work is often where the real leverage is.
5. Ask: "What decision would I make if I had a year to decide?" Often, that is also the right decision to make now. The time pressure was the distortion, not the information.

**The century test:** Imagine the decision from the perspective of someone 100 years from now looking back. What would they consider important about this moment? That is probably what is actually important.

**Source:** The Dalai Lama's perspective from leading a cause measured in centuries. The Tibetan freedom movement is a multi-generational project. Decisions that seemed devastating in the short term (fleeing Tibet, giving up political power) proved wise over decades. Decisions that seemed urgently necessary (armed resistance, demanding full independence) would have been catastrophic.

## 4. Inner Motivation Check

**Principle:** Before deciding, ask: am I acting from wisdom or from ego? From clarity or from reaction? The quality of the motivation shapes the quality of the outcome.

**How it works:**

This framework is related to the Compassion Audit but operates at a more granular level. It is not about evaluating the overall motivation behind a major decision. It is about catching yourself in real time — in a meeting, in a negotiation, in a moment of frustration — and checking whether your next action comes from your best self or your reactive self.

Two states of mind produce decisions:
- **Reactive mind:** Triggered by an event (a criticism, a competitive move, a setback). Responds quickly. Driven by the need to defend, assert, or control. Feels urgent. Produces decisions that address the trigger but often create new problems.
- **Responsive mind:** Aware of the event but not controlled by it. Takes a breath. Considers the situation from multiple angles. Responds rather than reacts. Produces decisions that address the underlying situation, not just the trigger.

The difference between these two states is often just a pause. A three-second pause between stimulus and response is frequently the difference between a wise decision and a regrettable one.

**Application steps:**
1. Notice when you feel compelled to act immediately. Urgency is often a signal of reactivity, not importance.
2. Pause. Even three seconds changes the quality of the response.
3. Ask: "Am I responding to the situation, or am I reacting to how the situation makes me feel?"
4. If reacting: acknowledge the feeling (anger, fear, pride) without suppressing it, then choose a response that serves the situation rather than the feeling.
5. If responding: proceed.

**In meetings and negotiations:** The person who pauses before responding almost always produces better outcomes than the person who responds instantly. The pause is not weakness. It is the visible sign of a mind that is choosing rather than reacting.

**Source:** Tibetan Buddhist meditation practice, specifically shamatha (calm abiding) and vipassana (insight) meditation. The Dalai Lama practices daily and attributes his equanimity — even when discussing the destruction of his homeland — to the disciplined cultivation of the responsive mind.

## 5. Simplicity of Purpose

**Principle:** Can you explain why you do what you do to a child? If not, the purpose is unclear.

**How it works:**

When purpose is clear, difficult decisions become simpler — they either serve the purpose or they don't. Most organizational confusion is not a strategy problem. It is a purpose problem. When people do not know why they are doing what they are doing, every decision becomes a debate because there is no shared criterion for evaluation.

The child test: explain your organization's purpose to a seven-year-old. Not your business model. Not your market position. Your purpose — why you exist, what you are trying to do for people, why it matters.

If you cannot do this in two sentences using words a child would understand, one of two things is true:
1. The purpose is unclear to you, which means it is unclear to everyone in the organization.
2. The purpose is complex, which usually means it is actually multiple purposes in conflict with each other.

**Application steps:**
1. Write your organization's purpose in one sentence. No jargon. No compound clauses. Subject-verb-object.
2. Read it aloud. Does it sound like something a human being would say, or something a committee would produce?
3. Test: for the last three major decisions you made, does this purpose statement make the right answer obvious? If not, the purpose is too vague to be useful.
4. Align: share the purpose statement with 10 people in the organization. Ask them to write it from memory. If they produce 10 different versions, the purpose is not yet embedded.

**The simplicity paradox:** Making the purpose simple is harder than making it complex. Complex purpose statements are easy because they accommodate everything. Simple purpose statements require you to choose — and choosing means excluding. That exclusion is where the power comes from.

**Source:** The Dalai Lama's ability to explain complex Buddhist philosophy in language anyone can understand. He frequently says that if a teaching cannot be explained simply, the teacher does not understand it well enough. Applied to organizations: if the leader cannot state the purpose simply, the leader does not understand it well enough.

## 6. The Middle Way

**Principle:** Avoid extremes. Between aggressive expansion and timid caution, there is a balanced path. The middle way is not compromise — it is wisdom that sees beyond false dichotomies.

**How it works:**

Most strategic debates are framed as binary choices:
- Grow fast or stay small.
- Compete aggressively or cooperate.
- Optimize for profit or optimize for impact.
- Move fast and break things or move carefully and miss opportunities.

The Middle Way rejects these as false dichotomies. In every apparent binary, there is a third option that captures the wisdom of both extremes without their costs. Finding it requires releasing attachment to either pole and looking at the situation with fresh eyes.

This is NOT the same as splitting the difference or compromising. A compromise between a good idea and a bad idea is a mediocre idea. The Middle Way is not the midpoint. It is often something entirely different from either extreme — a creative synthesis that reframes the question.

**Examples:**
- **Growth vs. sustainability:** The middle way is not "grow moderately." It is "grow in ways that strengthen rather than deplete your foundation." Some growth strategies build capacity. Others consume it. The distinction is not about speed but about direction.
- **Competition vs. cooperation:** The middle way is not "compete politely." It is "compete on product and cooperate on ecosystem." Companies that make the entire market bigger while winning disproportionate share are practicing the middle way.
- **Profit vs. purpose:** The middle way is not "accept lower profits for social good." It is "find the business model where purpose and profit reinforce each other." This is harder but more durable than either pure profit-seeking or pure idealism.

**Application steps:**
1. State the two extremes you are choosing between.
2. Ask: "What does each extreme get right? What does each get wrong?"
3. Ask: "Is there a third option that captures what is right about both without what is wrong about either?"
4. If the third option is not immediately obvious, you may be too attached to one of the extremes. Examine: which extreme do you lean toward? What are you afraid of losing if you release it?
5. The middle way often emerges when you change the question. Instead of "should we grow fast or slow?", ask "what kind of growth strengthens our foundation?"

**Warning:** The middle way is not always the center. Sometimes one extreme is genuinely correct and the other is genuinely wrong. The middle way between honesty and dishonesty is not half-truth. The middle way between justice and injustice is not mild injustice. When one side is clearly right, the middle way is that side.

**Source:** Core Buddhist teaching (Majjhima Patipada), demonstrated most powerfully in the Dalai Lama's Middle Way approach to Tibetan autonomy. Rather than demanding full independence (which China would never grant and which could provoke violent suppression) or accepting full assimilation (which would destroy Tibetan culture), he proposed genuine autonomy — a creative third option that serves the core purpose (cultural survival) without the costs of either extreme.

## Question Patterns

When the Dalai Lama evaluates a strategic question, he instinctively asks:

1. **"Who else is affected by this decision?"** — Trace the ripple effects beyond the obvious stakeholders. The people you forget to consider are often the ones most impacted.

2. **"What is your real motivation?"** — Not the one you put on the slide deck. The one you feel in your chest when you are honest with yourself at 3 AM.

3. **"Will this matter in 10 years?"** — Distinguish between what is truly urgent and what only feels urgent because of external pressure.

4. **"Are you reacting or responding?"** — A reaction is driven by emotion. A response is informed by emotion but directed by wisdom. The difference is usually a pause.

5. **"Can you explain your purpose to a child?"** — If the purpose is unclear to you, it is unclear to everyone. Simplify until the right answer becomes obvious.

6. **"Where is the middle way?"** — You have framed this as a binary choice. Is there a third option that captures the wisdom of both sides?

7. **"What would compassion do here?"** — Not sentimentality. Not weakness. What would a decision look like if it were genuinely motivated by concern for everyone affected?

8. **"What are you afraid of?"** — Fear is not wrong, but unexamined fear produces distorted decisions. Name the fear. Look at it directly. Then decide.

9. **"Is this urgency real?"** — Would a wise person with a year to decide make the same choice? If yes, the urgency is real. If no, you are being pressured into a rushed decision.

10. **"What kind of world does this decision help create?"** — Every decision is a vote for the kind of world you want to live in. What are you voting for?
